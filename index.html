Ciders

Basic Dry

This is just about the simplest hard cider you can make.

Ingredients
  • 3 gallons apple juice or cider
  • Red Star Cote de Blancs yeast
Measurments
  • Original Gravity: N/A
  • Final Gravity: N/A
  • Final ABV: ~5%
  • Conditioning Time: ~4 weeks
Brewing Process
  1. Pour cider in to your fermenting vessel.
  2. Add yeast. Use a starter if it's old or otherwise questionable.
  3. Once fermentation is complete, bottle. If using priming sugar tablets, use enough for strong carbonation. With average carbonation this turned out slightly too flat.
Notes

Well, I wasn't lying when I said it was simple.

Taste-wise, this was amazingly clean and crisp. I was so used to commercial, over-sweetened ciders that this was a real treat. The yeast makes it extremely dry so you can really appreciate the character of the raw ingredients, which in this case was just juice. I'd recommend not cheaping out on it.

Why are there no gravity readings and an approximate ABV? Well, I kinda forgot to take an initial gravity reading. I took several to make sure fermentation had stopped, but they were ultimately not useful for measuring the alcohol content. Eventually, I bought another bottle of the cider and took a reading from that, which *in theory* should be close enough, but there's no guarantee, this the approximate ABV.

I used Trader Joes unfiltered apple cider. The quality was fine, but since it's unfiltered the cider was ultimately extremely cloudy. Next time I'm definitely using pectic enzyme to balance that out.

Belgian Raspberry Cider

Work in progress.

Ingredients
  • 2 gallons apple juice or cider
  • White Labs Monestary Ale yeast (WLP500)
  • 24 ouces frozen raspberries
  • Pectic enzyme
Measurements
  • Original Gravity: 1.050
  • Final Gravity: N/A
  • Final ABV: N/A
  • Conditioning Time: N/A
Brewing Process
  1. Make a puree of the frozen raspberries, using juice to thin it as necessary for a relatively smooth blend, then add to the fermentation vessel.
  2. Pour remaining juice in to your fermenting vessel.
  3. Add pectic enzyme
  4. Pitch yeast.
Notes

Initially I had planned to use another Belgian yeast, but after a few months in the fridge puncuated by power outages the the starter didn't start. The local home brew store owner recommended the Monestary Ale yeast; he's been doing this longer than I have, so I'm inclined to trust his judgement.

Beers

So far I've really only done one beer and that came out of a kit, and I even screwed that up by diluting the boiled mash too much and wound up under-gravity. I'll build this out as I experiment more.
